The Catcher in the Rye by J.D. Salinger

A book review.

Posted by Anurag Aryan on November 30, 2014
Tagged with book review.

Title: The Catcher in the Rye
Author: J.D. Salinger
Publisher: Penguin Books
ISBN: 9780241950425
Genre: Classics/Literary Fiction
Pages: 192
Source: Personal Copy
Rating: 5/5

It’s one of those book you either love or hate, there’s nothing in between. The novel was included on Time‘s 2005 list of the 100 best English-language novels written since 1923,and it was named by Modern Library and its readers as one of the 100 best English-language novels of the 20th century. It has been frequently challenged in the United States and other countries for its liberal use of profanity and portrayal of sexuality. It also deals with complex issues of identity, belonging, connection, and alienation.

The Catcher in the Rye is about alienation, the main character Holden Caulfield wants to make connection with other people but almost every scene in the book shows him trying to do that and failing. It’s the story of Caulfield between a short span of him dropping out of Pency Prep to finally deciding to go home. He may come out to seem lazy, stubborn, immature, unfocused, untruthful, dangerously short-sighted and lost in his own world of unrealistic expectations but all that makes him connect better to the teenage mind and it’s this aspect which makes the book still relevant though it was first released in 1951, giving it a place on shelf of timeless books.

The book is all about the Holden’s dream of being “the catcher in the rye”. He is so against the concept of growing up and to giving into the pain and sadness that he sees as the result of becoming an adult that he wants nothing more than to spend his life protecting others from losing the innocence of childhood. Just like standing on the edge of some crazy cliff and catching everybody who’s ignorantly going to jump off it not knowing what’s on the other side of it.

The book got into quite a controversies. John Hinckley Jr. attempted an assassination on Ronald Reagan in 1981 and one of the books owned by him was the one written by Salinger. Mark David Chapman shot John Lennon, Chapman was arrested with his worn personal copy of the book, inside which he had written, “To Holden Caulfield, From Holden Caulfield, This is my statement”. What is it about this book that evokes such reactions, you might think. There’s nothing in the book, it’s all in you. The book just heightens your inner emotions and angst.
