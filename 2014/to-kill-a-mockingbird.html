To kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee

A book review.

Posted by Anurag Aryan on November 30, 2014
Tagged with book review.

Title: To Kill a Mockingbird
Author: Harper Lee
Publisher: Random House (2010)
ISBN: 9780099549482
Genre: Classics / Historical Fiction
Pages: 320
Source: Personal Copy
Rating: 5/5

Some books are just good as books. They are well-written piece with creative imaginary and the author making sure that the book entertains you. But you can’t confine the books like To Kill a Mockingbird in these boundaries. The book has all those quality along with the important messages dealing with racism and our social scenario which has impacted many people over the last five decades.

It’s a story about Scout and her older brother Jem‘s childhood in Alabama and how a series of events shook their innocence, shaped their character and taught them about human nature. The book is basically divided into two parts. The first part revolves around the narrator, a six-year-old girl named Scout, her older brother Jem and their friend Dill, from Mississippi, who visits Maycomb and stays with his aunt Rachel every summer. Scout describes the city as, “Maycomb was an old town, but it was a tired old town when I first knew it.” So they didn’t have much to do whole summer other than playing and wondering about the mysterious figure, Boo Radley. The Radleys were the “weird family” of town and had lots of rumor about them, and since no one had ever seen Boo Radley, the trio always wanted him to come out of his house across the street.

“They are certainly entitled to think that, and they’re entitled to full respect for their opinions, but before I can live with other folks, i have got to live with myself. The one thing that doesn’t abide by the majority rule is a person’s conscience”

-Atticus Finch

The second part of the novel follows Scout and Jem as they deal with the repercussions of their father’s court case. Atticus Finch is a lawyer who is representing an innocent black man, Tom Robinson, accused of raping the town drunk’s daughter. Atticus and his family endure all sorts of abuse from white townspeople who are livid with him for representing a black man. When Robinson is convicted by all white jury, even when it becomes clear that he was innocent, he tries to run from jail finally getting himself killed.

“I think there’s just one kind of folks. Folks.”

-Scout Finch

The name of the book is also very well-chosen, it represents killing of the innocents. Maudie tells Scout: “Mockingbirds don’t do one thing but make music for us to enjoy. They don’t eat up people’s gardens, don’t nest in corn cribs, they don’t do one thing but sing their hearts out for us. That’s why it’s a sin to kill a mockingbird.”

What moves To Kill a Mockingbird to classic status is its morality and ability to draw sympathy out of readers as much today as when it was written in 1960. The book deals with heavy issues of racism, oppression, injustice. Amazingly, it is able to handle these deep and sensitive areas without feeling depressing or preachy. Lee accomplishes this by making the narrator a child and allowing us to learn along with her.
